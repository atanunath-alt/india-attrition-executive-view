Key Insights
🌎 India Overall
Mar–Apr spike concentrated 73% of total attrition into just two months — driven by 3 mega-accounts alone accounting for $4.5M (25% of total)
The attrition pattern is heavily front-loaded: March ($7.0M) and April ($6.4M) together represent $13.4M or 73% of FY27 India attrition, with February and May contributing a combined $4.8M. Three accounts — Swiggy ($1.6M), Infiniti Retail ($1.6M), and Cholamandalam ($1.3M) — alone constitute 24.9% of total attrition, signalling that India's attrition risk is highly concentrated at the top rather than broadly distributed. The leading cause across the region is Business Change & Distress at $5.5M (30%), followed by Financial & Contractual at $4.8M (26%) — both of which suggest external economic headwinds rather than Salesforce-controllable factors. However, $2.7M (15%) is attributable to Bad Debt, which represents a collection and qualification risk that requires tighter controls.

📈 CMI
Financial & Contractual pricing pressure is the dominant driver — 67% of CMI's $4.9M lost to contract disputes and pricing changes
CMI attrition is concentrated in Mar–Apr ($4.9M combined), with Financial & Contractual accounting for $3.3M (67%). The top loss — Swiggy at $1.6M — was a pricing-change cancellation. Core ($2.6M) and Marketing Cloud ($2.2M) are nearly equal contributors, suggesting a cross-product exposure. Premier and Signature accounts together represent 82% of CMI's loss, making it a high-value, low-volume attrition problem: bad debt is minimal at just 2.9%, so churn here is volitional and potentially recoverable with better pricing governance.

🏭 FINS
Implementation failures and low product adoption — not pricing — are eroding FINS; MuleSoft alone at $1.0M signals a deployment crisis
Unlike other segments, FINS attrition is driven by Implementation (29.5%) and Adoption (22.8%), totalling over 52% of its $4.5M loss — a clear signal of post-sale delivery failure rather than commercial pressure. MuleSoft accounts for $1.0M (22%) within FINS, and HDFC Bank is the single largest MuleSoft loss at $0.94M citing "Lack of Internal Expertise." FINS is the most evenly distributed month-over-month (Feb–May range: $0.2M–$1.5M), suggesting a structural pipeline of churning customers rather than a one-time spike. Bad debt at 1.1% is negligible.

🔴 Basic
Basic tier's $8.2M attrition is India's largest — but 33% is bad debt and 42% of reasons are blank, masking the true churn picture
Basic carries $8.2M (45% of India total) from 847 accounts at an average of just $9.7K per account — a long-tail volume problem. Bad debt at $2.7M (33%) is the dominant data signal: one in three Basic dollars was simply never collected. 42% of attrition reasons are blank, the worst data quality of any tier, making it impossible to run targeted retention programs. ESMB ($3.6M) and TMTHRE ($2.6M) are the primary segments. The fact that February is actually the largest single month for Basic ($2.1M) — unusual compared to other tiers — may point to earlier contract year-end dates in the SMB population.

🎯 So What — Priority Actions
Three interventions would address 70%+ of India's controllable attrition
1. Pricing governance: Financial & Contractual ($4.8M, 26%) — especially in CMI and Signature — is the most recoverable bucket. Better pricing playbooks and negotiation guardrails at renewal could protect ~$3M.

2. Post-sale delivery: Implementation + Adoption failures ($2.9M, 16%) in FINS and Signature point to CS resourcing gaps. Early-lifecycle health checks on MuleSoft and large CRM deployments are critical.

3. ESMB credit qualification: $2.1M in bad debt from ESMB is a qualification risk, not a retention risk. Tighter credit checks and shorter payment terms at deal-registration can recapture this.
